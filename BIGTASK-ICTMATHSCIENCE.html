SCIENCE - CLIMATE CHANGE

What is Climate Change?

Climate change refers to significant long-term changes in the average weather conditions of a region, such as becoming warmer, wetter, or drier over several decades or longer. It includes global warming (the rise in Earth's average temperature) and its broader effects on the climate system.

Causes of Climate Change

The current rise in global temperatures is primarily driven by human activities, especially since the Industrial Revolution. The main causes include:

Burning fossil fuels: Coal, oil, and gas release greenhouse gases.

Deforestation: Reduces the number of trees that absorb carbon dioxide.

Agricultural and industrial activities: Emit methane and other gases.

Effects of Climate Change

Climate change has severe environmental impacts, including:

Extreme Weather: More intense storms, heatwaves, and droughts.

Wildfires: Increased frequency and severity due to hotter, drier conditions.

Glacier and Ice Loss: Thawing permafrost, shrinking glaciers, and declining sea ice in polar regions.

Habitat Destruction: Expanding deserts and loss of biodiversity.

Food and Water Scarcity: Declining crop yields and fish populations.

Rising Sea Levels: Threatening coastal communities.

Solutions and Activities to Address Climate Change

To mitigate climate change, individuals and communities can engage in the following actions:

Reducing Fossil Fuel Use: Using renewable energy sources like solar and wind power.

Afforestation and Reforestation: Planting more trees to absorb carbon dioxide.

Energy Efficiency: Using energy-efficient appliances and reducing electricity consumption.

Sustainable Transportation: Walking, cycling, using public transport, or driving electric vehicles.

Waste Reduction: Recycling, composting, and minimizing single-use plastics.

Advocacy and Education: Raising awareness and supporting policies that combat climate change.

Climate vs. Weather

Weather: Short-term atmospheric conditions in a specific place and time.

Climate: Long-term average of weather patterns in a region, typically over 30 years or more.

Five Main Climate Types

Tropical Climates: Hot and humid, found near the equator.

Dry Climates: Arid and semi-arid regions with little rainfall.

Temperate Climates: Mild summers and winters, found in mid-latitudes.

Continental Climates: Large temperature variations between seasons.

Polar Climates: Extremely cold with ice-covered landscapes.

MATHEMATICS - TRIGONOMETRY

What is Trigonometry?

Trigonometry is the branch of mathematics that studies the relationships between the sides and angles of triangles, particularly right-angled triangles. The word comes from the Greek terms:

"Trigonon" – meaning triangle.

"Metron" – meaning measure.

Right-Angled Triangles

Trigonometric functions are based on right-angled triangles, which have one angle measuring 90 degrees. The three sides are:

Hypotenuse: The longest side, opposite the right angle.

Opposite Side: The side opposite the given acute angle.

Adjacent Side: The side next to the given acute angle.

The Six Trigonometric Ratios

Sine (sin θ): Opposite / Hypotenuse

Cosine (cos θ): Adjacent / Hypotenuse

Tangent (tan θ): Opposite / Adjacent

Cosecant (csc θ): Hypotenuse / Opposite (reciprocal of sine)

Secant (sec θ): Hypotenuse / Adjacent (reciprocal of cosine)

Cotangent (cot θ): Adjacent / Opposite (reciprocal of tangent)
